Methods — Full Study Overview

Purpose

This exploratory survey measured attitudes toward cloning (animals and humans), perceptions of humaneness, and participants' ability to visually classify animals as "Normal" or "Cloned" from photographs. The aim was educational: to demonstrate data collection, analysis, and interpretation.

Participants

Convenience sample: N = 43 (high-school students). Recruitment was via classroom announcements with supervising teachers. Participation was anonymous and voluntary.

Materials

Four images are used (place these images in the same folder as this HTML file): pig.jpg (Normal), sheep.jpg (Cloned), naur.jpg (Cloned), dog.jpg (Normal). We attempted to match framing and lighting to reduce non-substantive cues.

Procedure

  1. Participants read a short description and gave consent where required.
  2. Participants answered two opinion questions: (1) Should animals be cloned? and (2) Is cloning animals humane?
  3. Participants viewed four images in randomized order and labeled each as "Normal" or "Cloned".
  4. Participants answered whether cloning research should be expanded to humans and could leave open text comments.

Data handling

Responses were exported to CSV, cleaned (typos normalized), and aggregated. Analyses included proportion z-tests, two-proportion z-tests, chi-square goodness-of-fit tests, 95% confidence intervals (normal approximation), and Cohen's h for effect sizes. Results are exploratory due to sample size and sampling method.

Limitations

Small convenience sample, limited image set, and potential cultural or knowledge biases (e.g., familiarity with famous clones). Results should not be generalized to broader populations.

Full Analysis & Recommendations

Summary

Detailed interpretation and statistical notes

The data show conditional support for animal cloning (Yes favored by a plurality but with many Maybe responses), stronger perceptions that certain animals (sheep, gaur) are cloned, and a majority opposing expansion of cloning research to humans. Statistical tests provided in the modals use the normal approximation for proportion tests and two-proportion z-tests for comparisons; chi-square goodness-of-fit tests compare observed category counts to equal distribution when appropriate.

Recommendations

  1. Increase sample size to improve power and enable subgroup analysis (e.g., by prior biology exposure).
  2. Include more images and balance stimuli across breeds and contexts to lower image-specific bias.
  3. Collect demographic metadata (age, sex, prior knowledge) to test moderators of attitudes.
