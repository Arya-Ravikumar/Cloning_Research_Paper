Survey Methodology

This research study was conducted to understand public perception regarding the cloning of animals and humans. Participants were randomly selected from various high-school grade levels and presented with a digital survey. The survey consisted of multiple-choice opinion questions and four image-based classification questions. Each participant was shown four animals—pig, sheep, gaur (naur), and dog—and asked to identify whether each one was a cloned or naturally born organism. The pig and dog images depicted normal (non-cloned) animals, while the sheep and gaur images depicted cloned specimens. No participant was informed beforehand which animals were cloned. The goal was to measure intuition, moral reasoning, and accuracy regarding cloned organisms. Data was collected anonymously to avoid bias.

Should Animals Be Cloned?

Analysis

A total of 32 participants responded to this question. The distribution shows a fairly mixed response, with 12 in favor, 7 opposed, 9 undecided (“maybe”), and 4 unopposed/neutral. This suggests that while support exists, there is significant uncertainty among participants, likely due to ethical, biological, and environmental concerns.

Is Cloning Animals Humane?

Analysis

The majority (21 of 32) believe cloning can be humane under certain conditions. However, 11 participants feel it is not humane. This reflects the complex interplay between scientific benefit and perceived suffering or ethical dilemmas experienced by cloned organisms.

PIG — Normal Animal

Analysis

With 20 identifying it as normal and 12 incorrectly labeling it cloned, participants demonstrated partial accuracy. This suggests that physical appearance alone is not a reliable indicator for public identification.

SHEEP — Cloned Animal

Analysis

Participants correctly identified the cloned sheep at a high rate (26 vs. 6). This may be due to unconscious association with Dolly the Sheep, the most famous cloned organism in history.

GAUR / NAUR — Cloned Animal

Analysis

With 24 identifying the gaur as cloned, recognition was strong. Participants may have used subtle morphological cues or simply uncertainty about exotic species to assume cloning likelihood.

DOG — Normal Animal

Analysis

Most participants (28 of 32) correctly identified the dog as normal. This likely stems from greater familiarity with dogs and recognition of typical canine traits.

Total Accuracy Across All Four Animals

Overall Analysis

Participants displayed strong recognition for familiar animals (dog, pig) and moderate to high recognition for cloned animals (sheep, gaur). Overall accuracy was above 65%, indicating that while appearance alone is not a perfect indicator, people may rely on intuition based on familiarity or assumptions about cloning.

Should We Expand Cloning Research to Humans?

Analysis

With 12 in favor and 20 against, respondents overwhelmingly reject human cloning research. Ethical, biological, and societal concerns appear to dominate perceptions of risk.
