Cloning Survey Research Project™

Public Perceptions of Animal & Human Cloning

Study Overview & Methodology

In this survey, participants were asked a set of questions to explore views on cloning. First, they answered whether they think animals *should* be cloned and whether they believe cloning is humane. Then, they were shown images of four animals — a pig, a sheep, a naur, and a dog — and asked to identify each as “normal” or “cloned.” Finally, they were asked whether cloning research should be expanded to humans. The survey was conducted anonymously, and respondents came from a diverse background in terms of age, educational level, and prior knowledge about genetic science.

Data was collected and categorized manually. Percentages were calculated to show proportional opinion rather than raw counts. The analysis sections below include both quantitative insights and interpretive commentary on what the data might suggest about public understanding, biases, and ethical sentiment surrounding cloning.

Should Animals Be Cloned?

Analysis

Most participants lean toward “Yes” or “Maybe” when it comes to cloning animals, showing openness to its potential but also some hesitance. The small “No” response hints at a minority who are firmly against cloning, perhaps on ethical or biological grounds.

Is Cloning Humane?

Analysis

A majority believe cloning can be humane, but a sizeable minority disagrees. This likely reflects moral concerns about animal welfare and the unknown risks associated with cloning.

Normal vs Cloned — All Animals

Analysis

When combining all species, a substantial share believed the animals were “normal” rather than cloned, suggesting participants had difficulty identifying cloned animals visually. This may point to general misunderstandings about how clones look — or to a perception that clones are nearly identical to non-clones.

Pig — Normal vs Cloned

Analysis

Since the pig was actually a normal (non-cloned) animal, the fact that most participants guessed “normal” is encouraging. It suggests at least some ability to recognize typical non-cloned characteristics.

Sheep — Normal vs Cloned

Analysis

The sheep was cloned, but many participants guessed “normal” — revealing a gap in visual detection. This may suggest cloned sheep look very similar to non-cloned ones in the image shown, or that people generally assume “sheep = normal.”

Naur — Normal vs Cloned

Analysis

For the naur, there was a more balanced split between “normal” and “cloned.” This may imply that the image was ambiguous to participants or had features that made some suspect cloning. It’s a useful insight into how recognizable (or not) clones can be.

Dog — Normal vs Cloned

Analysis

Because the dog was normal, the fact that most identified it correctly supports that cloning was not visibly obvious in this case. It indicates participants could trust their judgment more for this animal.

Should We Expand Cloning Research to Humans?

Analysis

Based on the responses (Yes: 6, No: 12), there is considerably more opposition to human cloning than for animals. This suggests people are more cautious about extending cloning technology to humans — likely due to stronger ethical, social, and biological risks.
