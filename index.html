Cloning Survey Research Project

Public Perceptions of Animal Cloning — Data Collection & Analysis

Project Overview & Methodology

This project explores public perception of animal cloning through a structured survey including ethical questions and identification tasks involving images of real cloned and naturally-born animals. Participants evaluated whether cloning should be allowed, whether it is humane, and attempted to identify cloned vs. normal animals based on images alone.

Results are presented below with charts, visualizations, and written analysis. All data has been manually calculated, converted into percentages, and presented with clarity and transparency for research integrity.

Should Animals Be Cloned?

Analysis

The majority of responses fall under “Yes” and “Maybe,” indicating uncertainty but general openness toward cloning. Only a small minority expressed firm opposition.

Is Cloning Humane?

Analysis

The split between “Yes” and “No” suggests a moral debate among students, with many unsure about the welfare implications of cloning.

Normal vs. Cloned (All Animals Combined)

Analysis

The difficulty identifying cloned animals visually suggests cloning does not produce easily recognizable differences, challenging common misconceptions about cloning.

Pig — Normal vs. Cloned Identification

Analysis

Most participants believed the pig was normal, indicating the cloned pig’s appearance was not noticeably unusual.

Sheep — Normal vs. Cloned Identification

Analysis

Responses suggest cloned sheep are also visually indistinguishable from normal ones, aligning with previous cloning research involving sheep.

Naur — Normal vs. Cloned Identification

Analysis

The naur had a more even split, suggesting subtle image elements made participants suspicious it might be cloned.

Dog — Normal vs. Cloned Identification

Analysis

Nearly all participants labeled the dog as normal, suggesting cloning did not alter recognizable traits in this case.
